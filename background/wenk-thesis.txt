Zusammenfassung
Eine eindeutige Interpretation und Auswertung von Kernspinresonanzdaten (NMR) zur
Aufklärung von chemischen Strukturen kann aufgrund vieler sich ergebener kombinatorischer
Möglichkeiten hinsichtlich der Konstitution zur Herausforderung für den organischen oder
Naturstoffchemiker werden. In der Vergangenheit kam es diesbezüglich zu mehrfachen
Fehlzuordnungen. Systeme zur computer-gestützten Strukturaufklärung (CASE) adressieren
genau dieses Problem. Sie sollen die Bestimmung unbekannter oder bekannter Strukturen für
den Nutzer möglichst vereinfachen und die Fehlerquote verringern. Im Rahmen dieses
Projekts wurde deshalb Sherlock entwickelt, eine frei zugängliche sowie quelloffene Software
zur computer-gestützten Strukturaufklärung.
Die Anwendung bietet sowohl die Prozessierung und Visualisierung von NMR-Daten als
auch den anschließenden Abgleich mit Einträgen aus einer spektralen Datenbank oder die
de-novo Aufklärung einer noch unbekannten Struktur. Letzteres geschieht unter Nutzung
eines Strukturgenerators und anschließender Sortierung der erzeugten Strukturen gemäß der
Ähnlichkeit ihrer vorhergesagten Spektren zum experimentellen Spektrum. Ergebnisse der
Aufklärung werden zudem im System abgespeichert und sind somit jederzeit wieder aufrufbar.
Die Prozessierung von 1D/2D-NMR-Daten ist mit den gängigen NMR-Dateiformaten
möglich. Der Funktionsumfang reicht hier von der Fouriertransformation bis hin zum Peak
Picking, wobei dem Nutzer mehrere Übersichtstabellen zum aktuellen Stand zur Verfügung
stehen, insbesondere die aller gepickten Signale sowie Korrelationen. Zusammen mit einer
gegebenen Summenformel enthält die Korrelationstabelle die Schlüsselinformationen, die für
den Strukturgenerator als Eingabedaten genutzt werden.
Zusätzlich führt das System bestimmte Abfragen in zuvor aufgestellten Statistiken
durch, um die möglichen Hybridisierungszustände und bestimmte Typen an Nachbaratomen
von Kohlenstoffen automatisch zu ermitteln. Neben dem Informationsgehalt aus den 2DKorrelationen und der Summenformel konnte dadurch zusätzlich eine deutliche Reduktion der
möglichen Lösungsmenge erzielt werden. Weitere Einstellungen und manuelle Änderungen
sind für den Nutzer zugänglich, um den Dereplikations- oder Aufklärungsvorgang zu
verfeinern.
Die Anwendung des Systems wurde anhand von 45 Testfällen demonstriert. Vierzig
davon konnten gelöst werden, wobei die zu erwartende Struktur zumeist an erster Stelle der
ausgegebenen Kandidatenliste positioniert war. Nur in wenigen Fällen mussten Parameter
angepasst werden. Die Erstellung und Nutzung von statistischem Wissen zur Ermittlung von
zum Beispiel von möglichen Hybridisierungszuständen oder verbotenen und obligatorischen
Nachbaratomen konnte eine deutliche Verkleinerung der Anzahl vorgeschlagener Ergebnisse
erzielen. Mithilfe der Fragmentsuche und Nutzung des ersten gefundenen Fragments konnte
insgesamt die Lösungsmenge in 34 von 40 Fällen auf ausschließlich die zu erwartende
Struktur reduziert werden. Fünf Testbeispiele konnten aufgrund ihrer Komplexität und
fehlender zusätzlicher experimenteller Daten in dem vorgegebenen Zeitfenster nicht generiert
und gelöst werden. Dies bedeutet jedoch nicht zwingend, dass Sherlock dazu prinzipiell nicht
im Stande ist. Mit fortführender Analyse mittels zusätzlichen strukturellen Informationen sowie
Expertise ist dies denkbar.

Dem Nutzer wird somit eine freie Software geboten, welche die gängigen Schritte der
Strukturbestimmung umfasst. Dies beginnt beim Einlesen der Ausgabedaten des NMRSpektrometers und geht über die (Roh)Datenverarbeitung bis hin zur Ausgabe von

vermeintlichen Abwesenheit einer solchen frei zugänglichen Software nicht möglich. Durch
die Veröffentlichungen von Sherlock durch Wenk et al. [1] konnte diese Lücke geschlossen
und der quelloffenen Gemeinschaft ein umfassendes Werkzeug zur computer-gestützten
Strukturaufklärung geboten werden.

VII

1. Introduction
The identification and elucidation of the molecular nature of small organic compounds
(<1500 Dalton) plays a key role in various research fields such as medicine and pharmacy [2–
8] or in terms of the biosynthesis of compounds and their derivatives in bacteria, plants, fungi
and other organisms [9–14].
The identification deals with the search for perhaps already known molecules in spectral
reference databases. This procedure is also named dereplication. Mass spectrometry (MS) or
one-dimensional nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy are popular choices. If the
database screening is successful and candidates based on spectral similarity can be
proposed, no further steps are necessary and the process usually stops here. Otherwise, the
more time-consuming acquisition of further analytical experiments and processing is required.
A subsequent de novo structure elucidation takes place. Both the identification and elucidation
here refer to already isolated compounds. [15–20]
The structure elucidation procedure is a so-called inverse problem [2,17,21] where the
compound's molecular structure is unknown and is inferred as the most probable candidate
from underlying measured and interpreted analytical data. NMR spectroscopy is therefore the
most important analytical method for structure elucidation. Its unique selling point among other
spectroscopic methods is that it provides atom-to-atom relationships and hence insights into
the close neighbourhood of atoms within the unknown under investigation. Furthermore,
continuous developments in the NMR technique in the last decades have allowed more and
more conclusions about the molecular skeleton of chemical compounds. [2,16,17,22–26]
A typical spectral collection for structure determination consists of at least a molecular
formula (MF) obtained by high-resolution MS (HRMS) and one-dimensional and homo- and
heteronuclear two-dimensional NMR data. The MF offers the elemental composition, i.e. the
number of hydrogens and heavy atoms (non-hydrogens) within the molecule. A specific
number of constitutional isomers, molecules with the same elemental composition but different
atom states and connectivities, can be generated from one MF. For example, ten isomers exist
in the case of the MF C5H10 while already more than 2.95 million structures form the
constitutional search space when adding six carbons with the resultant MF C11H10. Twodimensional NMR experiments provide specific connectivities between atoms and small
structural units. These structural restrictions typically already shrink the constitutional search
space enormously. [16,17,21,27,28]
One challenging aspect here is that the manual interpretation and linkage of the
structural units can be very slow and tedious for the chemist due to the still potentially high
number of combinatorial possibilities. Furthermore, this might lead to potential
misinterpretations or a non-exhaustive exploration of the solution space and thus to incorrect
structural assignments. For instance, from a pharmaceutical point of view, natural products
(NPs) and synthetic compounds are important in discovering new drug candidates against
cancer diseases. Hence, it is indispensable to know the correct structure to avoid potential
negative consequences in the case of medical treatments with newly developed drugs led by
incorrect starting points. Another example is determining the structural condition of natural
compounds with observed antimicrobial properties that can be used for various medical
purposes. [2,16,17,22,29,30]
The scenarios mentioned above are only two of the various conceivable ones and
indicate the importance of correctness in structure elucidation. Several articles [2,20,28,31,32]

1

state that there is a noticeable number of incorrectly assigned structures for NPs in the
literature and revisions were required.
The application of computer-assisted structure elucidation (CASE) software forms a
powerful approach to address this problem. Next to the already mentioned restrictions by twodimensional NMR experiments, one-dimensional NMR data is typically applied for spectral
database lookups to provide fully pre-connected substructures during the subsequent
structure generation. The exhaustive generation of all possible constitutional isomers
considering the limitations given by the spectroscopic and non-spectroscopic input is one
significant advantage when using CASE tools. The focus lies on reducing the solution space
to a small subset of plausible isomers which are in agreement with the constraints derived by
the interpretation of NMR data. Finally, the output of a CASE system contains a ranked list of
candidates with the most likely in the first place. [16,17,20]
Other analytical methods providing structural hints during the CASE process of novel
compounds are, for instance, infrared (IR) and ultraviolet-visible (UV). [16,26,33–35]
The history of CASE started already in the 1960s. The DENDRAL project [36] counts as
the first CASE approach. Several other expert systems with different implementations have
been developed. Examples are CHEMICS [37], LSD [38,39], LUCY [40], COCON [41],
SESAMI [42] or SENECA [33]. [16,17,27]
As Wenk et al. [1] indicated recently, today’s popular CASE programmes are either
commercial or closed software (e.g. ACD/Structure Elucidator [43,44], Mestrelab MNova [45],
Bruker CMC-se [46,47]) and not freely accessible for scientists or do not contain all utilities
needed for a comprehensive CASE system (e.g. Schmarnica [48], pyLSD [49,50] or
WebCOCON [41,51,52]). This includes spectral import, visualisation and processing, and
CASE-related functionalities such as dereplication or de novo elucidation. Hence, it was
reported by the authors that there seemed to be no non-commercial tool which offered these
two functional components at once and that there was a gap and a need to develop an open
and freely accessible comprehensive CASE system. By introducing Sherlock [1], a free and
open-source software for CASE offering the two core features described above, Wenk et al.
could close this gap. [1]
This manuscript is about the general idea of modern CASE systems and how the
implementation of Sherlock was done accordingly. First, a general description of an NMRbased computer-assisted structure elucidation approach takes place (second chapter). The
third chapter explains the structure and implementations within Sherlock and how individual
components in the backend and frontend work. The fourth chapter will discuss general
observations and results of single test cases. The last two chapters deal with a summary and
an outlook on future improvements or extensions.

2. Theoretical Basics
In the following chapter, basic knowledge will be explained to understand the
methodologies of further chapters. Here, analytical methods to obtain structural properties of
unknown organic compounds, mainly by means of NMR spectroscopy, and the general
principle of computer-assisted structure elucidation are dealt with.

2.1 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy
This is a powerful and non-invasive analytical technique for the determination of
chemical compounds. The basic principle is to take advantage of the inner magnetism of nuclei
with a spin-activity which is detectable by NMR (NMR-active). The nuclear spin is determined
by the number of neutrons and the number of protons in a nucleus. A nucleus is considered
magnetic when the atomic number and mass are not even. Typical nuclei under investigation
in organic samples are 1H, 13C, 15N, 19F or 31P. [7,53–57]
The application of a static magnetic field to the sample leads to the alignment of the
nuclear magnetic moment into a specific spin state α, a nuclear energy level. Electromagnetic
irradiation (radiofrequency, pulse sequences) brings the spin state α into another spin state β.
When the irradiation switches off, the nuclei enter the relaxation phase to reach the equilibrium
within the static magnetic field. This means the nuclear magnetic moment returns from spin
state β to the previous spin state α. During that phase, the excited nuclei show a resonance
by emitting electromagnetic waves recorded as free induction decays (FIDs) until the
equilibrium is reached. The FID represents the raw spectroscopic data in the time domain. A
Fourier transformation is then applied to convert the information into the frequency domain.
[31,53,54,57]
Different NMR data pre-processing steps exist, either applied in the time domain (FID)
or after the Fourier transformation in the frequency domain. For example, line-broadening is
used to minimise the noise ratio by a mathematical smoothing procedure within the raw data.
In the frequency domain, phase correction is applied to union the imaginary and real parts of
the complex spectrum so that the spectrum contains symmetric and slim peaks. Although
phase correction is meant to flatten the baseline, another pre-processing step, the baseline
correction, exists to correct the baseline distortions caused by corrupted data points in the
FID. Both correction methods are essential steps to retrieve well-shaped and offset-free
peaks. [53,58,59]
Surrounding electrons of a nucleus within a molecule influence the local static magnetic
field and span a shielding field (shielding effect). That causes a variation in the nuclear
resonance frequency and is known as a chemical shift. Electron-withdrawing groups around
a nucleus cause a deshielding and higher chemical shift value. In the opposite case, the
chemical shift is decreased (shielding). [54,55,57]
The shielding effect and, thus, the resulting chemical shift is the main benefit of NMR
spectroscopy since it gives characteristic insights into the local chemical and physical
environment of nuclei. In addition, chemical shift references, e.g. Tetramethylsilane (TMS),
are used to compare NMR spectra acquired with different magnet field strengths. Chemical
shifts are expressed in parts per million (ppm), whereas the TMS chemical shift is the origin
(zero). [53–55,57]

3

2.1.1 One-dimensional Experiments
One-dimensional (1D) NMR experiments consist of the information obtained by the NMR
experiment for one specific nucleus type. A 1D spectrum shows the chemical shift on the xaxis against the intensity on the y-axis (Figure 1).

Figure 1: Full 1H spectrum of Ibuprofen dissolved in CDCl3 (Chloroform-D1).

Signals derived by peak picking within NMR spectra provide a tuple of characteristic
attributes such as the individual chemical shifts, intensities and integrals of peaks, their
splitting patterns (multiplets) due to spin-spin couplings and coupling constants. For example,
chemical shifts can be used for structure identification or search for atomic properties and
molecular fragments by means of spectral databases (Section 2.3.2 and 2.3.4). A peak integral
value indicates how many protons for that specific frequency and nuclear environment exist.
The overall sum of integrals within a spectrum gives insights into molecular concentrations.
Splitting patterns (singlet, doublet, triplet etc.) bear hints about the neighbourhood of a nucleus
and coupling constants (Hz) about the distance (number of bonds) between two nuclei. The
latter is also expressed as nJ coupling, whereas n means the bond count. [1,16,53,55,57]
Figure 2 shows the cut-out of a proton (1H) spectrum example for the spectral pattern of
Ibuprofen’s aromatic group. The relative integral number of each signal leads to the
assumption of two represented protons respectively. The splitting pattern (doublet, d)

2.1.1.1 DEPT (Distortionless Enhancement by Polarisation Transfer)
Distortionless Enhancement by Polarisation Transfer (DEPT) experiments offer the
determination of the multiplicity (number of attached protons) of heavy (non-hydrogen) atoms.
They are combined with proton decoupling and use different pulse sequences, such as DEPT90 or DEPT-135, to obtain structural insights about bound CH (methine), CH2 (methylene) and
CH3 (methyl) groups. DEPT-90 spectra display exclusively methine groups, whilst DEPT-135
spectra represent methine and methyl as positive signals and methylene as negative ones
(Figure 4). This leads to the direct determination of methylene groups due to their appearance
as the only negative peaks. The additional information from the DEPT-90 spectrum enables a
distinction between methine and methyl units. Methyl units are present in the DEPT-135
(positive) but do not appear in the DEPT90 experiment. Hence, the quaternary carbons are
the remaining signals in the 13C spectrum (Figure 3) without any signal within both DEPT
experiments. [16,55,57,60]

2.1.2 Two-dimensional Experiments
Two-dimensional (2D) NMR experiments are the most important regarding
spectroscopic methods and structure elucidation. Different radiofrequency waves excite
different nuclei and thus allow the targeted application and distinction of specific nuclei and
their resonances. This enables the measurement of multiple nuclei in one experiment and
homo- and heteronuclear correlations through single (1J coupling) or multiple bond
correlations (nJ couplings) or through space (Nuclear Overhauser Effect) can be achieved.
Thus, due to the limitation of the number of bonds or spatial restrictions between atoms, those
experiments bear crucial structural constraints to narrow down the constitutional and
stereochemical space of a molecule under investigation. [16,17,20,57,61]
In contrast to 1D NMR spectra, both axes of a 2D spectrum show a chemical shift range
for each nucleus. It is usually displayed as a contour plot whilst the z-axis, seen from above,
represents the contour level (peak intensities) (Figure 6). [55,57]

7

2.1.2.1 HSQC (Heteronuclear Single Quantum Coherence)
The specific pulse sequences applied during the heteronuclear HSQC experiments
reveal 1JXH coupling information, which means direct bonds between protons (H) and heavy
atoms (X), mostly carbons and nitrogens (Figure 5). In addition, multiplicity-edited HSQC
records phase-sensitive information that operates similarly to knowledge derived from DEPT135 spectra. That means CH/CH3 and CH2 signals can be distinguished by their sign, positive
and negative, respectively. Hence, the assignments of protons to heavy atoms are provided
and the number of attached hydrogens can be set via DEPT or the phase information of a
multiplicity-edited HSQC. This leads to the first small structural units of a molecule. Another
source of single-bond information, similar to HSQC, is the HMQC (Heteronuclear Multiple
Quantum Coherence) experiment (Figure 6). [16,55,57]

2.1.2.2 COSY (COrrelation SpectroscopY)
The 1H,1H-COSY spectrum gives insights into the molecular skeleton of a structure
through the presence of J-resolved bond correlations. This NMR experiment type is
homonuclear and applied for detecting proton-proton interactions. The number of bonds is
typically two (2JHH, geminal) or three (3JHH, vicinal), see Figure 7. Those correlations are known
as standard correlations [61] whilst those whose distance exceeds such default distance (nJHH,
n > 3) have been introduced as non-standard correlations (NSCs) [61]. The presence of NSCs
may cause specific difficulties due to violating the basic assumption mentioned above (nJHH, n
= 2, 3). It may lead to no or wrong results during the structure generation process. Hence, if
known, NSCs must be explicitly treated in CASE expert systems. [16,17,20,61]
Diagonal signals represent the one-dimensional spectrum, whereas the symmetric offdiagonal signals (cross signals) indicate the correlating protons in the molecule (Figure 8). The
vicinal correlations allow a direct connection between the two heavy atoms assigned to their
corresponding protons. Hence, COSY data provide knowledge about the links between the
basic structural units derived by DEPT and HSQC/HMQC interpretation which usually leads
to an enormous shrinkage of the molecular search space. [16,17,40,55,57,61]

2.1.2.3 HMBC (Heteronuclear Multiple Bond Correlation)
The heteronuclear two-dimensional HMBC experiment (Figure 10) is another powerful
source of structural constraints. These long-range correlations are primarily measured for the
combination of proton (H) and carbon (X = C) or nitrogen (X = N), see Figure 9. Hence, unlike
COSY experiments, heavy atoms can be incorporated into the elucidation process without a
directly attached proton. The basic structural units of heavy atoms and attached protons
derived from the HSQC spectrum can be linked directly via 2JXH coupling (two bonds) or 3JXH
coupling (three bonds), both assumed as default distances here. NSCs (nJXH, n > 3) may
appear as well. Due to these limitations regarding distances between atom pairs, similar to
restrictions obtained by COSY data, the amount of combinatorial possibilities in terms of
molecular constitution continues to decrease with each such correlation between frequencies.
[16,17,20,40,55,57,61]

2.1.2.4 Further 2D Experiments
There are more NMR measurements to further support the structural analysis of a
chemical compound in terms of both constitution and stereochemistry. Despite their
usefulness, these spectral data are neither applied in this work nor specific methods are
completely implemented so far to handle and benefit from them. In this regard, no further
explanations or usages exist. Three examples are only quickly mentioned in the following
lines.
TOCSY (TOtal Correlation SpectroscopY) experiments can be applied to assist in the
rapid determination of substructures. This approach is based on detecting correlations
between all protons, not just vicinal ones, belonging to one whole spin system. It hence leads
to specific structural patterns, e.g. sugar rings. [16,62]
Another experiment type is the INADEQUATE (Incredible Natural Abundance DoublE
QUAntum Transfer Experiment). It provides one-bond correlations (1JCC) between carbon
atoms and most of the skeleton of an organic compound can be achieved by these direct
carbon-carbon bond information. Especially in the case of molecules with proton-poor regions,
that additional information is a crucial benefit to fill the gap of structural knowledge because
no further information can be obtained by typical proton-based NMR experiments. The major
drawback of this method is the very low natural abundance of the NMR-active 13C nuclei
(around 1,1%). Hence, only less of the neighbouring nuclei in a molecule are 13C and would
give a signal. Nevertheless, methods and their improvements exist to enhance that process
and to obtain signals, but they are often time-consuming. [16,26,57,63,64]
NOE (Nuclear Overhauser Effect) experiments, such as NOESY/ROESY, allow the
exploration of the stereochemical aspect of a molecule. Correlations are caused by spatial

11

interactions between protons and not by through-bond couplings, as in the 2D experiments
mentioned before. A proton-proton distance, usually not more than 5 Å, is expressed by the
peak intensity. Depending on that, these spatial relationships can be used to compute
stereochemical configurations. [16,20,55]

2.2 Further Analytical Methods
Next to NMR spectroscopy, different analytical methods provide insights about the
nature of chemical compounds or are applied to verify a result. This includes, for example, MS
and UV, IR or Raman spectroscopy. Similar to the NMR technique, UV, IR and Raman spectra
can also be used for database lookups to detect fragmental patterns within the molecule under
investigation, i.e. functional groups. MS can also provide structural information in this manner,
but moreover, it delivers a molecular mass and formula in high-resolution MS (HRMS).
Genomics and corresponding software can also reveal structural hints for natural products
under investigation. Combining those mutually independent analytical techniques and
gathering their specific meaningfulness allows a comprehensive and powerful CASE process.
X-ray crystallography forms another powerful technique for final structure verification and
determination. But it requires that a compound is crystallisable and that both the quantity and
the resulting crystal quality are sufficient, which is not possible in every case.
[2,9,10,16,17,26,32–35,65]
Although the additional information gained by the methods mentioned above is an
important cornerstone of CASE, this work solely deals with 1D/2D NMR data and a molecular
formula. With this in mind, this manuscript has no further explanations and application of these
additional experiment types.

2.3 Computer-Assisted Structure Elucidation (CASE)
A generally possible strategy of modern computer-assisted structure elucidation expert
systems will be described in the following. An overview is given in Figure 11 and serves as a
guideline within this chapter.

Figure 11: Flow chart of a conceivable, general approach for a CASE system with spectral data and a
molecular formula as input. A ranked list of structure proposals represents the output. Input and output
nodes are highlighted in blue.

Two typical approaches exist in modern CASE expert systems. First, the structure
identification and second, the de novo elucidation. The structure identification aims to avoid
the time-consuming re-elucidation of an already known chemical compound through
spectroscopic database lookups. In the de novo structure elucidation strategy, CASE systems
profit mainly from a molecular formula and through-bond correlations between atoms or atom
groups derived from 2D NMR data. Both serve as initial limitations concerning the
constitutional search space. Additional structural constraints, such as detected fragments or
the spectroscopist’s restrictions, can further reduce the solution set during the structure

13

generation process. A list of ranked structure proposals forms the output of such a CASE
system. Conventional NMR spectra considered during CASE are one-dimensional 13C, 1H and
DEPT as well as two-dimensional HSQC, HMBC and COSY data. Further experimental data
types were mentioned in Sections 2.1.2.4 and 2.2. [15–18,20,26,33,40,66,67]

2.3.1 Spectral Input and Processing
Most modern CASE systems expect a molecular formula as input and spectral data such
as NMR or IR. Both are obtained by the analytics of the same isolated compound under
inspection. Spectral processing tools are used to visualise, manipulate and interpret the input
spectra, e.g. NMRium [68] as a freely accessible and open tool developed for this purpose. A
converting step is required depending on whether one of the datasets is present in any raw
data format. For example, in the case of NMR data, a Fourier transformation is applied to
convert from the time domain (FID) into the frequency domain and, if necessary, some
manipulation or correction steps, as mentioned in Section 2.1. The subsequent peak picking
procedure gives a list of signals containing characteristic features that can be used for the
dereplication process if desired. Otherwise, the spectral interpretation and generation of basic
structural units are performed next. [16,17,20,66]

2.3.2 Dereplication
The dereplication is a database lookup for identifying previously elucidated chemical
compounds with the same or very similar spectral properties. The purpose is to avoid the
potentially time-consuming re-elucidation of a known molecule. 1D NMR, IR and MS data are
the typical experiment types since their recording is completed within a short time. Each record
in such a spectral database contains a spectral fingerprint for its assigned structure (structurespectrum correlation). That means atoms in a molecule are assigned to signals having
characteristic spectral features. This allows the search for spectral similarity hits between a
query spectrum from the CASE system’s input spectra and spectra within knowledge bases
(Figure 12). [15–18,20,26,67,69]

14

Figure 12: Exemplary scheme of a dereplication procedure with 13C NMR data of Ibuprofen as input.
After the peak picking, the obtained spectral properties (chemical shift, equivalence, multiplicity) are
used as a query spectrum. All records of a spectral database with structure-spectrum-assignments are
checked against the query spectrum for spectral similarity. The output is a list of matching database
entries.

Spectral binning is a method to create spectral fingerprints and can be applied as a prescreening within spectral databases. In principle, a spectrum's chemical shift range is divided
into sections (bins) of a common length. Each bin indicates whether at least one signal within
this chemical shift section exists or not, the value of one or zero, respectively (Figure 13). The
resulting bitstring is then used to calculate spectral similarities to other ones. One
measurement for this purpose is the Boolean AND, computed between the query bits and
each bitstring stored in the database. If the resultant bitstring is the same as the query bitstring,
then a complete spectral match and candidate structure is detected. Due to the bitwise
operators on an already pre-calculated set of bits, this procedure enables an efficient and fast
comparison and database screening to pre-select potential candidates concerning the spectral
input. [16,17,69,70]

15

Figure 13: Spectral binning example by dividing the 13C spectrum of Ibuprofen into sections (bins).
Each bin has an equal size of five ppm. The presence of at least one signal within a section is indicated
by the value of one or zero if not present. Red ones are part of the chemical compound’s signals. The
black one belongs to the solvent (CDCl3) peak at around 77 ppm.

Next to the spectral binning approach, distance measurements between signals on the
experimental and predictive side can be applied to enable a more flexible and precise type of
spectral similarity. Either the database candidates from the pre-screening through spectral
binning, which would speed up the process, or the whole knowledge base is taken into
account. This approach is based on forming signal pairs bearing the least chemical shift
deviation while considering additional parameters for finding a match, such as having the
same multiplicity or chemical equivalence. After detecting those signal pairs, an average
deviation, or any other statistical value, over all single chemical shift deviations between the
allowed signal pairs can be calculated and used for the subsequent solution ranking.
[1,16,17,69]
Signals of proton-broadband decoupled 13C spectra seem attractive for that purpose
because of their general sharp appearance as singlets, reproducibility and hence the easier
classification into bins or distance measurements. [16,17,71]
NMRShiftDB [67,72] is a free and open-source database with structure-property
assignments, mainly 13C, forming one candidate for a suitable spectral knowledge base. There
is also a recently published dereplication approach [19] demonstrating the usage of chemical
shift predictions for the LOTUS natural products database [73].
Exemplary commercial and closed-source representatives are CSEARCH [74], SpecInfo
[75,76] and ACD/Labs [77,78].
In case of results from the dereplication process, the detected molecules and their
assigned spectral properties will be forwarded to the solution ranking. The CASE system
continues with the basic structural unit determination if nothing can be found. This forms the
starting point of the subsequent de novo structure determination.

16

2.3.3 Basic Structural Unit Determination and Connectivities
The structure elucidation procedure takes place in case the dereplication is not
successful. In this step, the molecular formula (MF) and spectral information are used to build
the first pieces of the molecular skeleton. According to the given MF, all atoms are created as
single units. One-dimensional and two-dimensional NMR data are mainly exploited for the first
connections between them, as explained in Section 2.1. The interpretation of 1D, DEPT,
HSQC/HMQC data allows the assignment of protons and hence the construction of
fundamental fragments, for example methyl groups or amino groups. Those basic
substructures are then linked through the correlations gained by the analysis of 2D NMR
experiments, such as COSY or HMBC experiments. [15,16,26]
One network-based representation of the 2D correlations between the basic fragments
used in modern CASE tools is the molecular connectivity diagram (MCD) [16,34,44,45,61].

NMR spectra for the Ibuprofen case. Figure 15 demonstrates an MCD visualising these
correlations between basic structural units.

2.3.4 Fragment Detection
CASE systems also profit from spectroscopic database lookups by retrieving structural
fragments, the so-called goodlist and badlist elements [36,79]. The first symbolises
substructures that must be in each solution after the structure generation and the latter which
must not. The databases contain subspectrum-substructures-correlations [69] of previously
fragmented molecules and their assigned spectra. The screening is similar to the procedure
described for the dereplication process (Section 2.3.2). That means spectral similarity search
is used but with the difference that partial spectral matches within the query spectrum are
allowed (Figure 16). Such structural restrictions may also be added by human experts into
CASE systems to bring in a priori information. For example, when specific functional groups
or ring systems are suspected within the molecule under investigation. [15–17,80]

2.3.5 Structure Generation
The in silico molecular structure generation forms the core of the structure elucidation
approach. The collected structural information and restrictions gained during the previous
steps serve as input for a structure generator tool. A list of possible constitutional or
stereoisomers will be computed afterwards. This procedure depends on the chosen
systematic strategy, provided constraints and individually selected parameters. Constitutional
isomers are considered chemical compounds sharing the same molecular formula but vary in

18

the structural design of how the atoms are connected (Figure 17). Stereoisomers additionally
differ in their spatial arrangements. [15–17,27,34,66,81–83]
Examples of open-source molecular structure generators are Open Molecule Generator
[84], MAYGEN [85], surge [86] and enu [83]. The latter is also able to compute stereoisomers.
MOLGEN [87] is one example of a closed-source tool in this category.

Figure 17: Constitutional isomer example for C5H10. The ten possible constitutional isomers were
generated by means of the open-source graph generator surge (v1.0) without any additional parameters
or restrictions.

Graph theory is a common utility for in silico structure generation. Thus, molecules are
usually produced by graph generators and represented as chemical graphs. The latter
consists of nodes and edges representing the molecule’s atoms and bonds. A chemical graph
is denoted as a squared and symmetric connectivity matrix where the nodes are symbolised
as rows/columns and edges as numeric entries (Figure A76, Appendix). Regarding computerassisted structure elucidation (CASE), a given molecular formula initiates the set of nodes.
Edges can be set in the beginning according to determined fragments from 2D NMR or goodlist
elements. Further bond addition takes place during the subsequent graph generation process.
[16,17,27,81,83]
One systematic difference between structure generators is how molecules are built via
assembly or reduction. Structure assembly focuses on the stepwise addition of bonds between
atoms and bonds. Each created bond leads to a new starting point within the connectivity
matrix for further bond extensions so that, considering the spectroscopic and nonspectroscopic constraints, all possible chemical graphs can be generated. The structure
reduction strategy works in the opposite way. Initially, a large set of isomers, if not consisting
of all possible ones, is generated for a given atom set. Bonds will be gradually removed.
[15,17,27]
Another principle of how to build isomers is in a deterministic or stochastic manner.
Deterministic graph generation algorithms follow the strategy to generate all possible
molecular structures exhaustively. The higher the number of non-hydrogen (heavy) atoms, the
more combinations and thus larger chemical search space exist. This can lead to a
combinatorial problem and very long, unapplicable runtimes. [16,17,33,64,81]
The number of constitutional isomers using the molecular formula C5H10 is ten (Figure
17). This task has been finished within less than ten milliseconds. After increasing the
19

number of carbons to ten (C10H10), 310.248 structures were produced in 0.11 seconds. In the
case of C11H10, 0.82 seconds were needed to calculate 2.951.035 candidates and in the
case of C12H10, already 8.12 seconds were used to compute 30.051.331 structures.
Furthermore, the runtime for the Ibuprofen example (C13H18O2) was around six minutes with
5.971.088.592 possible molecules. These examples demonstrate the dramatic growth of the
chemical search space and computation time by adding a few heavy atoms to the molecular
formula. The graph generator surge (v1.0) with default settings and without additional
constraints was used for the structure generation.
Around twenty years ago, it has been reported that use cases usually did not exceed
the number of non-hydrogen atoms of more than 30 in deterministic CASE systems at this
time [17,33,88].
CASE systems aim to exploit spectroscopic and non-spectroscopic constraints to tackle
that issue by reducing the solution space [16,17,20]. These include, for instance, the following
CASE applications based on the assembly and deterministic structure generation approach
such as COCON [41,51,52], LSD [38,39,89], pyLSD [49,50], LUCY [40], Sherlock [1],
ACD/Structure Elucidator [34,43,44].
Complex problems bearing proton-deficient parts could lead to extensive calculations
due to the lack of signals and hence provide barely structural hints via using the conventional
set of 2D NMR experiments (1H,X-HSQC, 1H,X-HMBC, 1H,1H-COSY with X = 13C, 15N, …). In
this context, the so-called Crews’ rule states that determining the molecular composition from
conventional NMR experiments becomes difficult or impossible if the ratio of protons to all
carbons or heavy atoms is less than one or two, respectively. [2,20,23,24,90]
Database lookups for suitable fragments that cover a molecule's proton-poor regions
can be applied to close the structural knowledge gap and help in this situation. Subsequent
developments in computational power resources, algorithmic optimisations and access to
larger spectroscopic databases (primarily commercial or in-house) allow the structure
elucidation of unknowns even with up to 80 skeletal atoms using deterministic strategies,
assuming that the provided structural information about the molecule under investigation is
available and sufficient enough [16,80]. But the problem remains if no or not enough additional
constraints exist, e.g. substructures of proton-poor parts within a molecule could not be
identified due to their absence in the structure-spectrum knowledge base.
[16,17,20,33,36,64,79–81]
In that scenario, stochastic approaches form an alternative and were developed to
circumvent the combinatorial complexity which the deterministic approaches might have when
dealing with use cases described above. Heuristic methods such as simulated annealing [91]
or genetic algorithms [92] were implemented into different tools (e.g. SENECA [33,88], Genius
[93], SpecSolv [69]) to lead the generation process of the chemical compound towards
optimised results by defined scoring functions and optional backpropagation. Those
evolutionary procedures decide on potential successors of the current molecule state based
on their calculated fitness or energy score, so the most likely successors are selected and
considered for the next generation to be extended again. Furthermore, backpropagation
enables a step backwards to the ancestor's molecular connectivity with the background of
allowing the selection of another, maybe more promising successor or when a valid and final
structure is found. Hence, optimisations towards plausible solutions are feasible without
walking through the entire constitutional search space. [16,17,27,81,88]
Next to the approaches and given constraints mentioned above, additional specific
parameters can be taken into account to influence and guide the structure generation
20

procedure. Those parameters may be pre-set but modifiable at any time and might not
necessarily derive from spectroscopic analysis. Examples are a minimum or maximum ring
size within the molecule under construction or the specific exclusion of a certain atom count
in rings. Another setting is whether bonds between heteroatoms or charged atoms are
allowed. General chemical constraints, for example to follow Bredt’s rule [94], or default badlist
elements can be applied additionally to filter out chemically invalid or improbable constitutional
constellations. [16,17,31,61,88,89,95,96]

2.3.6 Spectra Prediction
After the structure generation procedure, when all plausible molecules are available, the
spectral prediction for each structure takes place. This step prepares for spectral ranking, the
subsequent and last stage of the general CASE strategy illustrated in Figure 11.
Among the different analytical methods, the estimation of NMR spectra turned out to be
a capable way to use for CASE. Empirical approaches such as the hierarchically ordered
spherical environment code [97], artificial neural networks [71,98,99] or incremental methods
[100,101] have been in use. Ab initio quantum-mechanical calculations are considered as
alternative prediction methods to the empirical ones. [16,17,66,102–105]
Accuracy and speed are two critical requirements for CASE systems, especially when
dealing with large molecules in the candidate list or when it contains thousands or tens of
thousands of candidates whose structures are in agreement with the spectroscopic and nonspectroscopic input. Quantum-mechanical (QM) simulations, such as those using the Density
Functional Theory (DFT) [106,107], are able to deliver precise results. But at the same time,
they are very time-consuming. This approach is thus preferably applied to make a final
decision for the most probable structure among a short list of structural proposals.
[16,17,71,96,104,108,109]
For instance, Trac et al. [110] selected the first three candidates from an already preranked candidate list. DFT calculations were done by means of the commercial tool Gaussian
[111] to further determine the most likely structure bearing the lowest chemical shift deviations
compared to the experimental input. The pre-ranking was achieved by faster empirical
prediction methods implemented in the CASE system in use. They will be described in the
following.
Incremental approaches are based on additive rules and equations to calculate chemical
shifts. The structure is divided into specific fragments (substituents), which serve as basic
units with initial chemical shift values from table lookups. Depending on the atom within the
selected fragment, pre-defined incremental values (positive/negative) of surrounding atoms or
substituents that do not belong to the selected one are used to quickly calculate an atom's
final chemical shift value by mathematical additions. [16,17,100,104]
Artificial neural networks (ANNs) consist of different models with a specific number of
layers and intercommunicating nodes (neurons). A model needs to be trained by sufficient
input data, e.g. through topological descriptors with corresponding spectral properties, and
multiple learning cycles in order to use it for reliable prediction purposes. Prediction requests
to neural networks deliver very rapid responses with accurate results. [16,17,71,99,104]
Hierarchically ordered spherical environment (HOSE) codes are well-known and
established structural line notations holding the capability of estimating moderate chemical
shifts and were adapted in several spectral databases and CASE systems, e.g. NMRShiftDB

21

[67,72], SpecInfo [75,76], CSEARCH [74,112], pyLSD [49,50], Sherlock [1] and ACD/Structure
Elucidator [44,113]. These topological descriptors are atom-centric, with spherical divisions
and thus describe an atom's neighbourhood in a spherical and canonical manner. The order
of nodes in one sphere depends on the substitution levels of their connected atoms in the
subsequent spheres. This is done by applying a specific weighted rule set, enabling such a
hierarchical ranking of atoms within every sphere. Chemical shifts of atoms are influenced by
their close neighbourhood and a fundamental assumption is that atoms sharing the same
spherical environment (HOSE code) also have similar chemical shifts. Hence, the associated
spectral properties of these atoms can be collected for the same HOSE code. Afterwards,
statistical values such as the mean value among all chemical shifts stored for a HOSE code
are calculated for chemical shift predictions. [16,17,49,66,97,103,108,114,115]
Figure 18 illustrates the spherical partitioning and atom-centred fragments defined by
HOSE code line notations. The HOSE code of the zeroth sphere contains the central atom,
while the next HOSE code also bears the first sphere's three carbons. From the second sphere
onwards, a distinction and ranking between the three carbons in the first sphere can be made.
The carbon connected to the two aromatic neighbour carbons is the first, while the carbon
bearing the two oxygens is in a lower order (second position). This is due to pre-defined
substructure codes and corresponding weights. For example, the code for the aromatic
property has a higher weight than a double bond. The methyl group of the first sphere forms
the last placement because it is not connected to any other successor and hence has the least
weight.

Since increment methods, ANNs and HOSE codes are empirical strategies and
dependent on previously collected data, erroneous or missing entries might lead to imprecise
and insufficient results. Spectral databases bearing a high number of chemically diverse
records are, therefore, fundamental for accurate predictions. In contrast, QM simulations (nonempirical) come with precise results too. Still, next to the drawback of having expensive
calculation times, the choice of a suitable QM function and parameters for the simulation might
be obscure. It may influence the result to a remarkable degree. Empirical methods work
automatically in that sense and do not require any further settings. [16,17,66,69,71,104]
Stereochemical aspects can be considered during spectral estimation. QM simulations
are able to calculate and take into account spatial conditions within molecules. HOSE codes
were modified to consider stereochemistry, for example applied in CSEARCH-STEREO [112]
and the stereo-enhanced HOSE code approach by Kuhn and Johnson [114]. By using
appropriate input-output pairs containing spatial information, ANNs can be trained and thus
can predict accordingly. [16,17]
Chemical shift predictions are mainly applied for but are not limited to 13C and 1H as they
are the most important nuclei in structure elucidation and verification through 1D and 2D NMR
experiments. Next to chemical shifts, signals in proton-broadband decoupled 13C NMR spectra
(Section 2.1.1) bear mainly singlets, making the prediction and subsequent comparison with
an experimental spectrum simple. In contrast, the additional spectral features of proton
signals, such as higher multiplets and coupling constants, lead to more complex predictions.
Furthermore, stereochemistry determines the chemical shifts of protons and the prediction
requires additional spatial information to enable a reasonable result. [16,17,114]
Two-dimensional NMR spectra can be predicted based on the one-dimensional spectra
[16,116] and used for substructure search, dereplication or determination of chemical
compounds [117,118].

2.3.7 Solution Ranking and Output
Spectral properties for atoms or groups of chemically equivalent atoms are estimated
and assigned to all plausible structural candidates during the previous step to make structurespectrum-assignments available. Those are then used for a spectral comparison with the
experimental observation to find the most similar and, thus, most probable structures among
all plausible solutions. Such a spectral similarity-based ranking can be achieved, for instance,
by using the average deviation of all single deviations between matching signal pairs (query
versus estimation). In the end, the CASE expert system outputs an ordered list of structural
proposals with the most likely one in the first place (Figure 11). [15–17,20,65,69,80]

A general strategy of a CASE system is described in the previous chapter. This one
explains the details of one implementation of such a CASE tool, namely Sherlock, introduced
by Wenk et al. [1].
An overview of Sherlock is given in Figure 19. This free and open-source system is
divided into two main components: a graphical user interface (GUI, frontend) and a backend
system consisting of different task units. Both components work independently and offer a
comprehensive CASE system in combination. The frontend enables parse, display and
process NMR data in several formats. Afterwards, the gained key information, i.e. correlational
data and molecular formula, and corresponding settings are forwarded to the CASE-related
services in the backend for the dereplication or the de novo structure elucidation. Sherlock
accepts one- and two-dimensional NMR data and an MF as input. The result is a list of ranked
structural candidates, which are, in case of an elucidation procedure, additionally stored in the
system and thus accessible at any time. [1]

Sherlock’s backend service comprises several sub-services for different tasks (Figure

the fragment DB service (via the dataset DB services). The result retrieval task uses the result

constraints will be used as input for the structure generator, which is part of the core service.

Sherlock has been containerised and is connected to other services via Docker Compose in

3.1.2 Structure-to-Spectrum Format and Knowledge Base
In order to enable database-dependent CASE features, such as the dereplication
(Section 2.3.2) or fragment search (Section 2.3.4), a structural collection coupled with spectral
properties was needed and had to be constructed. The requirement for database entries was
chosen as follows. Each record consists of a complete assignment of the 13C spectrum to
carbons in a structure. That includes the chemical shift, the number of attached protons
(multiplicity) and the equivalence count for a signal. The latter was determined by identifying
the identical chemical shift value and multiplicity within the same spectrum. If such a signal
already exists, then its equivalence count will be increased by one. Finally, the sum of all the
individual equivalence counts has to be the same as the number of carbon atoms in the
assigned compound. In addition, some metainformation, e.g. the molecular formula or the
spectral fingerprint, was added to the current record before storing it in the database. [1]
An example of the structure-to-spectrum assignment is shown in Figure 21. Due to their
chemical equivalence, the atoms 2, 3 and 7, 8 and 9, 10 are grouped and assigned to one 13C
signal, respectively. The signal equivalence count is therefore set to 2 in each case.

Figure 21: Structure of Ibuprofen with atom numbering (a) and carbon atoms in Ibuprofen with
associated spectral properties (b).

One source used for building the spectral knowledge base, which provides structure-tospectrum information, is NMRShiftDB [67,72] (version from 2nd of March 2022). Since one

26

molecule in NMRShiftDB could bear more than one 13C spectrum, each was extracted and
stored separately as one single dataset in Sherlock. This includes experimental and predicted
spectra. No differentiation during the database filling was made here. The number of inserted
database entries from NMRShiftDB is 27.938. [1]
The knowledge base was extended to increase diversity and reliability. Therefore,
COCONUT [121] was used as a second data source. COCONUT is, at its current state, a nonspectral database but with a high number of freely accessible natural products. Onedimensional 13C spectra were predicted for all molecules in COCONUT (April 2021) to obtain
the needed spectral information. The prediction was made using the carbon-13 prediction tool
in Advanced Chemistry Development, Inc. (ACD/Labs) [77,78]. Structures without and with
different stereochemical configurations were considered here. 864.903 structure-spectrumassignments could be added to Sherlock’s dataset database so that, in total, 892.841 records
exist. [1]
The computational library casekit [122], based on the Chemistry Development Kit
[123,124], was used to parse input files from NMRShiftDB and COCONUT and convert them
into the needed data structure. A design description of each spectral knowledge base record
is available in Table A14 (Appendix).
The structure-to-spectrum records are accessible through the dataset DB services
(Figure 20). The dataset DB instance runs a MongoDB [125] server holding and managing the
records. The DB service container requests on the DB instance and communicates to other
backend services, e.g. the core service.

3.1.3 Dereplication
As described in Section 2.3.2, this procedure performs a database lookup to figure out
whether known datasets can be found. Spectral similarity matching can be applied using the
experimental 13C query spectrum against spectra in the structure-to-spectrum knowledge
base. The aim is to prevent a re-elucidation of an already known chemical compound or to
retrieve similar ones. [15–18,20,26,67,69]
The following parameters for the database search are supported: the chemical shift
tolerance, maximum average deviation and the additional options to filter by molecular formula
or the identical multiplicity and equivalence count. [1]
The procedure starts with a MongoDB request to the dataset backend service, which
expects at least a fully described 13C query spectrum (Figure 21). Then the database will be
screened for the query spectrum’s nuclei and the signal count properties. Depending on
whether the dereplication settings include verifying the molecular formula, a database entry
has to hold the identical molecular formula. This step serves as an additional filter to speed up
the database lookup and pre-filtering.
Further processing of the hit list is applied by spectral matching. First, chemical shift
deviations between all signals from the experimental spectrum and the spectrum of one
database hit are built. The following conditions must be satisfied to detect valid signal pairs.
The first and obligatory check is whether the chemical shift difference is not larger than the
given tolerance value, which is two ppm by default. Also, the multiplicity and equivalence
properties have to be the same. These last two conditions are natively activated but optional
and can be skipped if desired. Signal pairs which pass these checks are then seen as valid.
Afterwards, the closest valid signal pairs in terms of chemical shift deviation are taken so that

27

they can be used to calculate the average deviation between all of them. This value must not
exceed the allowed maximum average deviation, which is pre-defined as one ppm. [1]
A distance matrix for the spectral matching procedure explained above is applied. Only
valid signal pairs are inserted into that matrix and used to form minimal distance pairs. An
illustration is given in Table 2. There, the chemical shift tolerance is increased from two to

Next to the dereplication, one of the main features of Sherlock is the de novo structure

3.1.4.1 Detection
Next to the information given by NMR experiments, CASE systems generally profit from
additional structural constraints to further reduce the possible chemical search space during
structure generation due to the allowance or prohibition of specific structural properties or
fragments (Section 2.3). [15–17,80]
According to this, Sherlock has several detection functionalities that aim to find and
provide certain structural constraints by looking into different statistics or a fragment database.
Both were built based on Sherlock’s structure-to-spectrum knowledge base. [1]
The statistics used for hybridisation, neighbourhood and hetero-hetero bond allowance
determinations are stored in a MongoDB instance within the statistics services. The fragment
search is done through a PostgreSQL [126] database instantiated in the fragments services.
3.1.4.1.1 Hybridisation States
One possibility to support the structure generation with helpful information is to provide
one or at least a reduced set of hybridisation states for each heavy atom of the unknown. The
spectral database entries in Sherlock contain the complete structure-to-spectrum assignment
for every compound and its elemental composition derived from the molecular formula. This
information is used to build a statistical basis. [1,16,80]
The hybridisation statistic bears specific key configurations: the nucleus, chemical shift,
multiplicity and elemental composition. Every record stores the occurrences (frequency) for
each observed hybridisation in that key configuration. [1]
Each entry within the structure-to-spectrum database is used to collect the hybridisation
counts and their specific key configurations. This is done by walking through every molecule
and its carbon atoms with structural and spectral properties, which are a part of the current
key configuration. Every hybridisation state is inserted into the MongoDB instance for
statistics.
The system expects the following parameters for a hybridisation detection query:
nucleus, multiplicity, minimum and maximum chemical shift, threshold value and molecular
formula. Minimum and maximum shifts form a tolerance window and are automatically
computed using an additional tolerance value set to two ppm. That means the minimum lies
at the query shift minus the tolerance and the maximum at the query shift plus tolerance. The
threshold value determines the minimal observed frequency of a particular hybridisation
compared to the total amount among all observed hybridisations for that key configuration.
The default threshold value is 0.01, equal to at least 1% of all occurrences. Every hybridisation
which reaches that lower frequency boundary is added to the output list. [1]
This enables a request for a 13C query signal or any other nucleus if available in the
database. An example can be seen in Table 3. There, a chemical shift of 113.19 ppm, a singlet

Another methodology in Sherlock offers the statistical determination of atoms which
have to be (mandatory) or must not (prohibited) be neighbours of a given signal by the query

directly bonded heavy atom neighbours of carbons and adds the amount of each. An entry in

compositions are deposited and increased for every new occurrence. [1]

3.1.4.1.3 Allowance of hetero-hetero Bonds
To provide the user with more statistical support, an additional implementation in
Sherlock exists to propose whether hetero-hetero bonds should be permitted during the
structure generation or not. [1]
To build such a statistic, the algorithm walks through every entry in the spectral
knowledge and connections between directly bonded heavy atoms are counted. These heavyheavy atom pair counts will be stored and linked to the molecular composition of the current
molecule. In the end, the total sum of all occurrences for every found heavy atom pair and
corresponding elemental composition is saved and available for requests.
The service assumes a molecular formula and a threshold value set to 1% to query. The
system will take all entries according to the given formula. Afterwards, it checks whether the
ratio of the sum of all hetero-hetero-atom pairs to the number of all detected bond pairs is
greater than the threshold. The method returns the Boolean value true if the minimal
abundance threshold is reached, otherwise false.

3.1.4.1.4 Fragment Library
Next to substructural information derived from 2D NMR, an additional input of fragments
to structure generators can lead to an enormous reduction of the candidate list since such
substructures either have to exist (goodlist) or are prohibited (badlist) in each solution. When
dealing with compounds containing proton-poor parts and thus hardly giving connectivity
information in such regions, goodlist fragments could help to overcome that gap of structural
knowledge (Section 2.3.4). [1,15–17,36,79,80]
The implementation for building fragments (substructure-subspectrum correlations,
SSCs [69]) is inspired by the approach by Elyashberg et al. [16] (chapter 7.4.1). One
substructure is constructed in a spherical manner by using a breadth-first search. Each nonhydrogen atom within a molecule is considered a central atom and starting point. The
fragmentation algorithm traverses every molecule in the database and checks whether bonds
should be preserved to keep important structural patterns. The first condition to preserve
bonds is in case they connect hetero atoms. Second, bonds with higher order than single are
not removed. Third, when carbon is connected to more than one hetero atom, that connection
will not be cut. Additionally, each ring or ring system is considered a complete starting point to
avoid destroying important ring information. [1,16]
If none of these conditions applies, a connected atom will be removed and replaced by
an R as a pseudo atom symbolising an open site. The maximum number of spheres is set to
three. A maximum ring size must also be provided for detecting rings and fused ring systems
utilising the Cycles.all() and RingSearch() methods within the CDK. Only rings bearing a
maximum number of six heavy atoms are considered here. When an entire ring system, either
34

a single or fused ring, forms the starting point and not just a single atom, another spherical
limit is considered. The system allows a search radius of one sphere around such a ring
system but still follows the three bond-preserving conditions mentioned above.
Fragmentations starting at different atoms within one molecule may produce the same
substructure. The system, therefore, checks for duplicates after each partition of a molecule
and removes them if detected. All spectral assignments for remaining atoms in a substructure
are kept during the partition process so that a unique list of substructure-subspectrumcorrelations is the output. [16]
The methods in the Fragmentation class within the casekit were used for the
fragmentation procedure.
Figure 25 demonstrates the fragmentation procedure by means of the Ibuprofen

sphere, whereas the maximum distance for all other starting points is three spheres unless
one of the three bond-preserving conditions described above is true. The latter is visible in
Figure 25g, where the two oxygens lie in the fourth sphere but are kept because the third rule

query bitset is created and used for a pre-screening against all bitsets in the fragment
database. A fine search is executed afterwards to filter out valid signal-signal pairs considering
more conditions and parameters (Section 3.1.3). [17,69]
Before the screening process starts, every surrounding bit of a set bit within the query
bitstring will also be set to the value true. This enables a tolerance frame and avoids a potential
missing of hits in case of very close chemical shift values between query and fragment, which
were put into two different bins. This may happen when both signals are close to the border
between two bins while one has a slightly lower chemical shift and is put into the first bin. The
other is assigned to the next bin because of a slightly higher chemical shift than the border.
An example of a spectrum containing tolerance windows is given in Figure 26. Here, the
signals at 44.9 ppm and 45.03 ppm are very close but exist in different bins. For cases like
this, to not miss a potential spectral hit, the tolerance frame mentioned above is applied during
the database screening.

A Boolean AND is applied during the database search and bitset comparisons to select
valid spectral hits. The resultant bitstring has to be the same as the fragment’s, meaning that
every set bit of the database entry is included in the query bitstring. [1,17]
Figure 27 contains an illustration with a query bitstring build from the Ibuprofen example
and an SSC in the fragment database. A Boolean AND operation between query and SSC
bitstring computes the result. The bitstring comparison is successful in this case because the
result bitstring bears the same set bits as the ones from the SSC. If the SSC contains any
other set bit that is not part of the query bitstring, then the Boolean AND operation leads to a
zero value for that specific bin in the resulting bitstring. Hence, an inequality in regard to the
SSC bitstring would exist and the SSC would be discarded.

Afterwards, the allowed and remaining fragments are processed similarly to during
dereplication (Section 3.1.3). This fine search is done by the computation of chemical shift
distances and valid signal pairs to decide on the most similar spectral assignments. The
system thus expects a parameter for the allowed chemical shift deviation and the maximum
average deviation. The parameters for checking the multiplicity and equivalence value for each
potential signal pair are set to true. Another step is to test whether the hybridisation of a
matching atom-signal pair within a fragment is included in the list of detected or provided
hybridisations for the query signal. If yes, or the hybridisation state list is empty, then this
signal-signal pair is finally valid. After these revisions are passed, some metainformation, e.g.
the average deviation, will be added to the fragment data. Finally, a fragment is then inserted
into the output list. [1]
Structurally identical fragments are gathered via their SMILES and the one with the
smallest average deviation among the structural duplicates is chosen to stay in the output list.
The candidate list contains all valid substructure-subspectrum-correlations and will be ranked

3.1.4.2 Structure Generation
The structure generation forms the core part of the computer-assisted structure
elucidation. Here, all of the collected (non-)spectroscopic information and restrictions are
considered limitations during the structure generation to shrink the constitutional solution
space as much as possible. This process takes place within the core service (Figure 22).
PyLSD [49,50], a free-to-use CASE tool and Python-based extension of Logic for
Structure Determination (LSD) [38,39], is integrated into the backend system and serves as a
structure-generating module. [1]
This CASE tool is able to handle ambiguous atom states, i.e. if no clearly defined
hybridisation states or an inconcrete number of attached protons for heavy atoms exist. Thus,
pyLSD addresses a critical part of CASE. Clear definitions for hetero atom states, for example,
might not be obvious to the spectroscopist. At that point, pyLSD takes over that task.
[49,50,127]

39

Figure 28: Flow chart of the structure generation procedure in Sherlock.

Figure 28 shows a flow chart of the structure elucidation task. The structural constraints
and settings are expected as the first input. Sherlock uses the default filters for general 3- and
4-membered rings provided by LSD if desired and custom filters that can be stored in the
backend system. Those filters are the second input for pyLSD and operate as badlist elements
(Section 2.3.4) to prohibit specific structural patterns in the solutions. Regarding creating a
pyLSD input file, converting the incoming information into pyLSD/LSD commands is needed.
The structure generator will interpret those commands to create every constitutional isomer
which is in agreement with the given structural limitations. All generated molecular candidates
are then collected from the output file after the pyLSD execution and passed towards the next
module for spectral prediction and ranking. [1]
3.1.4.2.1 Input Data and Settings
Several steps to prepare and assist the structure generation process were described in
the previous sections. All gained hints, either provided by human experts or detected or
derived from two-dimensional NMR experiments, are gathered to form the most
comprehensive collection of structural knowledge and restrictions about the unknown. This is
used as input for the structure generator. [1]
Different expected parameters and settings must be passed to this procedure. A brief
overview is given in Table A15 (Appendix).
3.1.4.2.2 Filter List
The possibility exists to store custom badlists in Sherlock's backend system.
Furthermore, two default filters for detecting 3- and 4-membered rings are already included
and thus available during the runtime. The latter is taken from LSD’s Filters directory and used
within the core service. Custom filters are automatically applied when they are recognised in
the backend service. The inclusion of the 3- or 4-membered ring filters depends on the
provided settings. Every activated filter will be forwarded to the responsible module for creating
the pyLSD input file described next.
3.1.4.2.3 PyLSD Input File
As a Python-based extension of LSD, pyLSD handles ambiguous atom states by a
systematic creation and execution of multiple LSD tasks with different atom state
configurations. The number of LSD input files depends on the complexity of the problem to
solve in terms of incomplete atom state definitions and the given molecular formula. [49,127]

40

Afterwards, pyLSD gathers all the different results into one set of solutions. Sherlock
parses the solution file to process it further.
The elucidation package within the casekit is used to build pyLSD files. The following
elements are currently supported: C, N, S, O, I, F, Cl, Br, P, Si.
The following summarises which pyLSD/LSD commands for an input file are
implemented. A short description for each command can be found afterwards. More details
are available in the pyLSD/LSD manuals [39,50]. A complete example of Ibuprofen example
is given in Appendix (A4).
First, the molecular formula is set through the FORM command. Afterwards, the PIEC
command is set to the value one while the optional ELIM command is disabled by default.
Atom states are defined by the MULT section, either provided by statistical detections, custom
values or default ones. Identical command names declare HSQC/HMQC, HMBC or COSY
information. If fixed bonds are given, they are added through the BOND property. LIST and
PROP are used to create lists of atom groups and to allow or forbid next neighbours. Provided
fragments (goodlist) and enabled filters (badlist) are written in temporary files and used by
LSD via the DEFF and FEXP commands. SHIH and SHIX commands are also defined based
on the chemical shift information for protons and heavy atoms to differentiate between
chemically equal and unequal atoms.
FORM
The FORM command expects the molecular formula of the isolated compound and
serves as the first upper boundary in terms of the solution space.
Example: ”FORM C 13 H 18 O 2”. Molecular formula of C13H18O2.
PIEC
This parameter influences the solution connectivity and means the number of
connected pieces of the result. This value is always set to one so that only fully connected
molecules are produced by default.
Example: ”PIEC 1”.
ELIM
This command is implemented in Sherlock in order to handle and eliminate invalid or
non-standard correlations. It first expects a number of correlations to delete and second a
number of bonds between the atoms involved in such a correlation.
Example: ”ELIM 1 4”. Eliminate one NSC with a distance of up to four bonds.
Eliminations are only possible when the minimum and maximum number of bonds are
explicitly provided and one of them exceeds LSD’s default upper boundaries for HMBC (> 3J)
and COSY (> 4J) correlations. See commands for HMBC and COSY signals below.
MULT
A MULT command declares an atom and its state. An atom number, the element
(optionally with valencies), potential hybridisation state(s) and number(s) of attached protons
41

are valid inputs. If none of this information is available, which means neither automatically
detected nor manually entered, then the default values (Table A16, Appendix) will be set in
the pyLSD input file. This is especially the case for hetero atoms for which no statistical
knowledge about structural properties is available. Hence, in the case of oxygen, the
possible hybridisation states are sp2 or sp3 and a multiplicity of zero or one. Furthermore, to
ensure that all available valences for atom types defined in pyLSD are taken into account,
Sherlock sets all of them into the input file.
Example 1: ”MULT 1 C 3 3”. The atom with index 1 is an sp3 hybridised carbon with a
multiplicity of 3. In this case, the atom state is unambiguous.
Example 2: ”MULT 19 N35 (1 2 3) (0 1 2 3)”. The atom with index 19 is nitrogen with
possible valencies of three or five. The hybridisation state is either sp1, sp2 or sp3. In
addition, the number of attached protons ranges from zero to four. This is an example of an
atom state with ambiguity.
HSQC
The HSQC command is used to assign protons to heavy atoms. Chemically equivalent
protons, e.g. within a methyl group, are listed as the same, whereas diastereotopic
hydrogens are provided in different assignments.
Example 1: ”HSQC 1 1”. The first heavy atom is connected to the first proton or group
of equivalent protons.
Example 2: ”HSQC 1 1” and ”HSQC 1 2”. The first heavy atom is connected to the first
proton and the second one. Diastereotopic hydrogens are assigned in that way.
BOND
Directly connected heavy atom pairs are defined by this keyword.
Example: ”BOND 1 2”. The first atom is directly bound to the second atom.
HMBC/COSY
These two keywords define HMBC and COSY correlations. A non-hydrogen (HMBC)
or hydrogen (COSY) atom is the first parameter, followed by the correlating proton. The next
two parameters are optional and declare a minimum and maximum distance (number of
bonds). The default is two and three, respectively.
Example 1: ”HMBC 1 3”. The first atom correlates with the third proton via two or three
bonds.
Two additional properties of HMBC or COSY signals within Sherlock can be passed,
determining the minimum and maximum distance or path length. The ELIM parameter, if
activated, eliminates only correlations which count as invalid and contain at least one of the
distance values higher than the default values. The default number of bonds in LSD is two
(minimum) or three (maximum) for HMBC and three for COSY.

42

Example 2: ”HMBC 1 3 2 4”. The first atom correlates with the proton with an index of 3
through two, three or four bonds. The latter is a non-standard correlation and ELIM has to be
enabled to take this into account.
Furthermore, the first parameter may consist of multiple atom indices. This enables a
certain flexibility so that one of these atoms correlates with the proton (second parameter).
This is useful when signals with very close chemical shifts can not be distinguished clearly
and hence it is ambiguous which of these signals correlate with that proton. All atoms of the
same group derived from the grouping information (Section 3.1.4.1.5) serve as input here.
Example 3: ”COSY (1 2) 5”. The first or second proton within one detected group
correlates with the fifth proton through three bonds.
SHIH/SHIX
By integrating these commands, atoms with identical atom states but different chemical
shifts are not treated as equivalent anymore, thus, allowing a distinction of such atoms during
the structure generation process.
Example 1: ”SHIH 1 1.12”. The first indexed proton or group of protons (e.g. CH3) bears
the chemical shift value of 1.12 ppm.
Example 2: ”SHIX 1 21.97” and ”SHIX 2 22.34” with ”MULT 1 C 3 3” and ”MULT 2 C 3
3”. Both carbons are assigned to identical atom states but bear distinguishable chemical shifts.
This combination leads to unequal atoms during the isomer generation.
LIST/PROP
LIST and PROP are integrated to prohibit or force atoms or atom types to be directly
connected neighbours of other atom groups.
Example: ”LIST L1 14 15” and ”PROP 1 0 L3 -”. The first defines a list L1 containing
atoms 14 and 15. The latter forbids the atom with index one to be connected to atoms in L1.
DEFF/FEXP
DEFF is a command to define fragments or point to files containing a (generic)
fragment description. FEXP combines the limitations through DEFF commands in a logical
manner.
Example file content of a generic 3-membered ring adapted from LSD’s filter list [39]:
“SSTR S1 A (1 2 3) (0 1 2 3)
SSTR S2 A (1 2 3) (0 1 2 3)
SSTR S3 A (1 2 3) (0 1 2 3)
LINK S1 S2
LINK S2 S3
LINK S1 S3“
This file is then defined as F1 via “DEFF F1 ‘/data/lsd/PyLSD/LSD/Filters/ring3’ ” and
with the command line “FEXP ’NOT F1’ ” such a 3-membered ring will not be allowed in the

43

resulting candidates. Logical (Boolean) chains of prohibited and mandatory substructures
are possible through a logical AND or OR between defined fragments.
3.1.4.2.4 PyLSD Execution
The construction of the pyLSD input file is followed by the execution of the installed
pyLSD instance within the core service. Temporarily files by pyLSD/LSD are written into the
container’s file system and tagged with an internal identification number so that multiple
tasks can run in parallel.
When an isomer generation process has been finished, the results are stored in the
SMILES format and parsed into Sherlock’s data structure. Here, the spectral properties and
structure-spectrum-assignments are not available yet. Hence, the next step is to predict and
assign those missing spectral features.
The integrated spectral estimation and solution ranking in pyLSD are disabled and
replaced by another prediction method described in the next section. [1]

3.1.4.3 Spectral Prediction and Ranking
In order to estimate chemical shifts, the usage of libraries consisting of HOSE codes [97]
and associated properties, i.e. statistics about assigned chemical shift values, has been
approved as a powerful empirical method (Section 2.3.6). Kuhn et al. published a so-called
Extended or 3D HOSE code [114], which takes advantage of stereochemical information, if
given, to allow the construction of different HOSE codes and thus a distinction between
stereochemically different parts of molecules. Sherlock uses the stereo-enhanced HOSE
code. [1]
Similar to what has been described in the literature [16,17,67,72,74,112,115], the
creation of the HOSE code database in Sherlock was done by traversing the existing data in
the spectral knowledge base. Every carbon atom in a molecule was taken as the central atom
to build the HOSE code with a minimum and a maximum number of spheres, one and six,
respectively [1]. The assigned chemical shift value for each central atom was appended to the
list of the same HOSE encoding and the current solvent of the assigned spectrum.
Afterwards, the number of entries as well as the minimum, maximum, mean and median
values for all collected HOSE codes and different solvents were deposited in the database.
The statistics services are responsible for prediction-related tasks and hold the HOSE
code statistics. Casekit and its HOSECodeShiftStatistics class were used to assist in the
creation of stereo-enhanced HOSE codes and statistics. The total number of entries in the
HOSE code knowledge base is around 6.3 million.
After the structure generation, when parsing the produced molecules, HOSE codes for
every carbon atom of each structure are built and used for the 13C spectrum prediction. The
number of spheres starts at six and is iteratively reduced by one if no record in the HOSE code
library for that specific encoding could be found. [1]
If a HOSE encoding is known in the database, then the average of every median value
of each solvent is calculated and used as a prediction value. An example is given in Table 7.
Due to the usage of the stereo-enhanced HOSE code by Kuhn et al. [114], the HOSE codes
contain explicit hydrogens.

During the prediction, every created structure-spectrum assignment will enter a filtration
step. A prediction will be kept or discarded according to given settings during a spectral
matching between a prediction and the query spectrum. The parameters are first an allowed
maximum chemical shift difference for determining matches and second the permitted
deviation in average. This matching procedure is the same as applied during the dereplication

3.1.5 Storage and Retrieval
When an elucidation task is successful and molecules could be generated, Sherlock’s
backend service will store the result. Only the first 500 results are deposited through
Sherlock’s result service and database (Figure 22). [1]
The content of each new record covers the correlation data, all the parameters/settings
and the list of structural candidates with predictions, assignments and further metainformation,
e.g. the date, an identifier and task name. The latter two allow the search for results by their
identifier or custom task name. With this, Sherlock does not only provide the retrieval of results
for previously run elucidation tasks. Furthermore, it allows re-running them using the same
spectral configuration but maybe with different parameters or structural constraints. [1]
Due to current technical limitations, the original spectral input data is not deposited in
the backend service. Hence, only the data mentioned above is retrievable.

4.2.1 Parameter Adjustments
4.2.1.1 Dereplication
As mentioned earlier, parameter changes in test cases 8 and 9 were necessary to
achieve the two further spectral database hits (Figure 53). [1]
In regard to test case 8, both the allowed deviation (DEV) and the allowed average
deviation (AVGDEV) had to be increased from 2 to 3 ppm and from 1 to 3 ppm, respectively.
Afterwards, every query signal has a valid partner in two database hits, so the expected
compound could be identified in both NMRShiftDB and COCONUT (Figure 54). The maximum
deviation between the query and the first result (NMRShiftDB) lies at 2.21 ppm and the second

74

(COCONUT) at 2.97 ppm. Both cause the increased DEV value. Green highlighted deviation
numbers symbolise a value of less than one ppm.
The experimental data was dissolved in Dimethyl Sulfoxide (DMSO), while the database
hit in NMRShiftDB was dissolved in Deuterium Oxide (D2O). The solvent used for the spectrum
estimation for the COCONUT entry is unknown. Since the environmental circumstances can
highly influence the resonances during an NMR measurement [130], solvent effects and hence
different resonances might be one explanation for those chemical shift differences. Other
experimental conditions might have influenced the result too.

Figure 54: Result of the dereplication for Citric acid (test case 8) with different search parameters than
the default ones. Identifiers in NMRShiftDB (left) and COCONUT (right) can be found at the bottom of
each result card. The two chemically equivalent carboxyl groups and their structural and spectral
assignments are highlighted.

Regarding test case 9, both the DEV and the AVGDEV had to be increased to 10 ppm
and two ppm, respectively, to achieve a dereplication in both COCONUT and NMRShiftDB.

Elucidation results for case studies 6, 10 and 30 are shown in Figure 61. In test case 6
(Figure 61a), the maximum single deviation was 5.55 ppm. In test case 10 (Figure 61b), it was
7.98 ppm. There were observations with higher deviations that are not allowed anymore, such
as the framed estimation value in test case 30 (Figure 61c), and hence stay unassigned. This
could lead to a lower rank in the result list because the backend returns the candidates ranked
by the number of matching signals first and then by the average deviations. It means the more
valid signals can be assigned, the higher the probability of being similar because that criterion
determines the order in the first place.
In addition, chemical shift estimations depend on the database used for HOSE code
library creation (Section 2.3.6). The number and diversity of molecules with assigned spectral
properties play a crucial role in prediction quality. But even with relatively diverse and large
input data, HOSE code entries can contain big chemical shift ranges. This may happen,
especially in lower spheres (Section 4.1.4) but also in higher ones, e.g. the signal at 55.31
ppm with six spheres in Figure 61a. There, the chemical shift value was determined
statistically through an average of representative medians of broadly distributed values. Thus,
a default chemical shift allowance window of +/- 10 ppm was selected to deal with those
situations when the prediction differs significantly and to build complete spectrum-spectrum
assignments as far as possible. The maximum average deviation threshold filters additionally
but considers the entire set of spectral matches, affecting the result generally.
The user also has the opportunity to influence the spectrum-spectrum matching
procedure by modifying the allowed tolerance and average deviation in the frontend. If the
permitted tolerance value is chosen to be lower or higher than the default value (10 ppm), then
81

less or more signal-to-signal assignments can result. In case of a lower tolerance, the
expected structure could be ranked lower due to a smaller amount of spectral hits with the
query spectrum. For such a scenario, the frontend’s result tab also offers to sort the result list
by average deviation instead of spectral hits count (Figure 40, Section 3.2.2.1). The expected
candidate might occur in higher ranking again afterwards.
The setting of these spectral prediction parameters is not the only factor which can
influence the estimation quality and result. Next to the data diversity, the availability and
consideration of stereochemistry during the HOSE code library creation and prediction
procedure is fundamental to distinguish between diastereotopic atoms (Section 3.1.4.3).
Diastereotopic carbons might not be distinguished by their 3D HOSE code since the
stereochemical information in the output of pyLSD is not provided and no method in Sherlock
is implemented to achieve it [1]. This results in generating the standard two-dimensional HOSE
code information and identical chemical shift prediction. Consequently, those diastereotopic
carbons belong to the same signal with an increased equivalence count within the predicted
spectrum. This causes a partial mismatch with the query spectrum because the equivalence
count has to be the same. Hence, the comparison will fail.

Figure 62: Elucidation result for test case 20 (3-hydroxy-drimenol [29]) as an example of erroneous
spectral prediction and mismatch. The blue circles indicate diastereotopic carbons for which the same
HOSE code and spectral properties were assigned. The corresponding signal with the resultant higher
equivalence count is framed in blue. Hence, one signal of the marked diastereotopic carbons is missing
in the prediction. This leads to a mismatch between the query and predicted spectrum, so the two
signals do not match.

One example is given in Figure 62. The two diastereotopic carbons were seen as
identical during the spectrum estimation and both were grouped into one signal with an
equivalence count of two. But two separate signals in the experimental spectrum existed, each
with an equivalence of one. Hence, the spectral matching procedure found no valid match in
the query spectrum. Consequently, thirteen of fifteen query signals could be assigned, while
two remained unassigned.
The issue described above could be solved if the structure generation module in
Sherlock would be able to compute the stereochemical properties necessary for the 3D HOSE
code generator to produce distinguishable structural encodings. Furthermore, the structureto-spectrum assignments used for the HOSE code knowledge base must contain this
information. Concerning that, not the entire input data includes the required information. The
COCONUT input data consists of planar entries as well as potential stereoisomers. The latter
provides the necessary stereochemical configurations, as exemplified in Figure 63. In
NMRShiftDB, not all database records bear the wedge bond tags and this does not guarantee
complete distinctions between diastereotopic carbons during the HOSE code knowledge base
construction. Further openly accessible structure-to-spectrum data could help to overcome
this problem in the future [1].
But even when this gap could be closed, the bottleneck of not having the necessary
three-dimensional information for molecules from the structure generation result remains.
Consequently, no proper spectrum-spectrum matching and prediction for diastereotopic
carbons can be ensured so far. As mentioned in Section 2.3.5, enu [83] is able to compute
constitutional as well as stereochemical isomers. It is thus one potential tool to inspect and
perhaps consider for future releases of Sherlock.

The HOSE code generator is able to differentiate between atom positions around a

Searching for fragments formed the next step. Fifteen substructures have been proposed with

again for that problem case, no result could be produced in a decent time. Hence, further

Although the fragment search results are ranked by spectral similarity, the selection of
fragments to use during the structure generation process has to be done manually. It is not
obvious to the user which fragment is proper and which is not without any expertise or preknowledge of the compound under construction. As described for Actinosiprol A and earlier
for test examples 9, 30 and 37, the selection of the first fragment might fail. Including additional
spectroscopic methods and filters could therefore be the next step for Sherlock’s spectral
databases and detection routines. One example is extending the existing fragment library by
records bearing proton NMR spectra. The goal is to reach more specificity during the search
due to the filtration on two levels, 1H and 13C, instead of only one. Another approach could be
to extend Sherlock’s functionality by the inclusion of structure-to-spectrum assignments for IR
or UV data which offer characteristic insights into substructural patterns (Section 2.2).
One prerequisite for potential extensions like this is that corresponding data is publicly
and freely accessible to import and use in open-source systems like Sherlock. Another aspect
is a certain minimum amount and diversity of such datasets. NMRShiftDB, for instance,
contains several thousands of freely available proton spectra, which could also be used in
Sherlock for spectral processing purposes.
In cases like Actinospirol A and Rubterolone A, taking custom fragments into account
offers one opportunity to bring in structural a priori knowledge and to cover certain parts of the
unknown. This can be useful, for example, when spectroscopists work with known or expected
substance classes containing characteristic functional groups or specific ring systems.
Furthermore, INADEQUATE experiments (Section 2.1.2.4) could help to overcome the gap of
structural knowledge when using commonly applied two-dimensional NMR experiments based
on proton interactions and thus are insufficient. The user could manually enter those direct
carbon-carbon connections through the dialogue for editing forbidden or mandatory
neighbours in Sherlock’s frontend (Figure 42, Section 3.2.2.2).
The authors of [9] (Actinospirol A) and [10] (Rubterolone A) have successfully used the
combination of multiple analytical techniques such as HRMS, NMR, genomics and X-ray as
94

well as cheminformatic tools to determine the constitution and stereochemical configuration
for the two isolated compounds under investigation. Different one- and two-dimensional NMR
techniques were taken into account to determine planar molecular fragments and connections.
Especially the direct carbon-carbon bond information from the INADEQUATE experiment
could give crucial insights into the highly substituted parts of the Rubterolone A. Furthermore,
NOE data was used to figure out stereochemical properties. In the case of Actinospirol A, the
DFT calculation tool Gaussian was applied for that purpose. Both final configurations could be
verified utilising X-ray analysis. [9,10]

5. Conclusion
An unambiguous interpretation and evaluation of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)
data for the elucidation of chemical structures can become a challenge for organic or natural
product chemists due to the many combinatorial possibilities that arise regarding the
constitution. In the past, there have been multiple misassignments in this regard. Systems for
computer-assisted structure elucidation (CASE) address precisely this problem. They should
simplify the determination of unknown or known structures for the user as much as possible
and reduce the error rate. Therefore, Sherlock, a freely accessible and open-source software
for computer-assisted structure determination, was developed within the framework of this
project.
The system offers both the processing and visualisation of NMR data and the
subsequent comparison with entries from a spectral database or the de novo elucidation of an
unknown structure. The latter is done using a structure generator and subsequently ranking
the built structures according to the similarity of their predicted spectra to the experimental
spectrum. Results of the elucidation are stored in the system and can thus be retrieved at any
time.
Processing 1D/2D NMR data is possible with the common NMR file formats. The range
of functions extends from Fourier transformation to peak picking, with several overview tables
available to the user on the current status, particularly those of all picked signals and
correlations. Together with a given molecular formula, the correlation table contains the key
information used as input data for the structure generator. Furthermore, the system performs
specific queries in previously built statistics to automatically determine possible hybridisation
states, certain types of neighbouring atoms of carbons and more. In addition to the restrictions
derived from the two-dimensional correlations and the molecular formula, the statistical
proposals significantly reduced the solution set. Different settings and manual changes are
accessible to the user to refine the dereplication or elucidation process.
A demonstration of the system's capability has been done by means of 45 test cases.
Forty of them could be solved and the expected structure was mostly placed at the top of the
resulting candidate list or at least among the first ten. Parameter adjustments were only in a
few cases required. The creation and use of statistical knowledge to determine, for instance,
possible hybridisation states or forbidden and mandatory neighbouring atoms could
significantly reduce the number of proposed results. Through the fragment search and the
application of the first fragment, the solution set could be reduced in 34 of 40 cases to solely
the expected structure. Due to their complexity and lack of additional experimental data, five
test examples could not be generated and solved in the given time frame. However, this does
not necessarily mean that Sherlock is unable to do so in principle. With further analysis using
additional structural information and expertise, this is conceivable.
Hence, a free tool that covers the typical steps of structure determination of small organic
molecules is offered to the organic chemistry or natural product community. The process
begins with reading the output data of the NMR spectrometer and continues through (raw)
data processing to the listing of structure suggestions. It could be shown that even test cases
with forty heavy atoms could be successfully validated in a few seconds without changing any
pre-set parameters.

96

A comprehensive comparison between Sherlock and other modern CASE systems with
a similar scope of functions is not possible due to their commercial nature or their absence in
the open-source community. The release of Sherlock by Wenk et al. [1] closed this gap to
provide a comprehensive and freely accessible CASE system to the community.

97

6. Outlook
The overall performance of Sherlock could be demonstrated in this work. No changes to
pre-configured settings were required for most of the first forty test cases to produce and rank
the expected candidate at the top of the output list. While for simple problems only a few or
solely the expected structure was in the solution set, some more complex cases led to a longer
candidate list, i.e. when fragments were not taken into account. Examples are Allantofuranone
(test case 22) or Cocaine (problem case 28).
Additional filters, such as integrating and considering 1H NMR spectra during the
spectral prediction, filtering and ranking, could help to reduce the candidate list. Freely
available proton NMR spectra exist in NMRShiftDB and could be considered for that purpose.
The fragment search and ranking can also profit from these extended specific spectroscopic
constraints. The first prototypic methods to deal with and filter by 1H and 13C (or any other
nuclei) are already implemented in the casekit. Corresponding optimisations and subsequent
adaptations in Sherlock’s frontend and backend system could be made in future, as already
indicated by Wenk et al. [1].
Furthermore, other open-access datasets with spectrum-structure assignments can be
considered and integrated into the system’s spectral knowledge bases and derivatives. The
aim is to increase the amount and diversity of data, e.g. used during the dereplication or to
improve spectral predictions. One example is the LOTUS dataset [19,132], providing a large
number of natural products and predicted 13C spectra calculated from different stereochemical
configurations. Another example of an NMR data source is the Biological Magnetic Resonance
Data Bank [133], from which experimental and calculated NMR data of the category Small
Molecules could be used.
Especially in cases like Actionspirol A with high proton deficiency, implementations
towards the inclusion and automatic interpretation of further NMR experiments than the
conventional ones (1D data and HSQC, HMBC, COSY) to obtain more structural insights
would be beneficial for the presented CASE software. Although INADEQUATE information
can be displayed in the frontend’s Spectra tab, the interpretation and setting of direct bonds
between carbons in the CASE tab is still the user's responsibility. In addition, handling more
specific pulse sequences, such as LR-HSQMBC [134], could be taken into account. It has
been demonstrated that highly proton-poor problem cases were solved by applying such
experiments [20,134].
The CASE system would also profit from implementations to support spectral data other
than NMR. For example, the detection of functional groups within the unknown compound
from IR or UV database lookups (Section 2.2). With this, other sources of structural constraints
would be available. [16,17]
Scoring systems like the Natural-Product-Likeness-Score [135,136] classify compounds
as NP or not. Such a scoring could be included in the solution ranking and NP-unlike
candidates might be placed in lower ranks or even discarded from the output list.
The determination of three-dimensional aspects of candidates through NOE
experiments has been mentioned in Section 2.1.2.4. Sherlock is currently unable to interpret
this kind of data. Other methods to deduce or predict stereochemistry are also not
implemented so that the resultant structures consist of two-dimensional coordinates only [1].
As discussed in Section 4.2.3, if this gap could be closed in future releases and distinguishable

98

stereo-enhanced HOSE codes can be produced, then the spectral prediction and ranking
procedures should be more precise.
Another conceivable scenario is that the user could select a number of candidates in the
result list and start QM calculations to achieve stereochemical configurations and chemical
shift predictions. A subsequent re-ranking of the solutions in accordance with the experimental
data would be possible. The user then receives a list of candidates bearing stereochemistry
and a potentially improved ranking. For instance, NWChem [137,138] is a freely accessible
and open-source computational chemistry tool which supports DFT-based NMR property
predictions and thus could be integrated as an additional microservice in the backend system.
The atom exchanges (combinatorics) required during the structure generation of
Ibuprofen (Section 4.3.1) and other problem cases might not provide a solution in complex
situations where atom substitutions are necessary on both axes. The currently available but
not applied methods in casekit and Sherlock need to be improved further.
Besides this combinatorial approach, there is also the opportunity to adapt several CASE
parameters or recursively exclude specific correlations until results can be produced [17].
Additionally, methodologies trying to detect non-standard correlations [16,61] and use
fuzzy structure generation [16,139] are also conceivable to include in the CASE process.
A study case result containing tautomers in the solution set was briefly discussed in
Section 4.2.3. One future implementation to assist the user could be to group detected
tautomers and show the most probable and representative molecule in the candidate list view.
A click on that molecule expands the view so that all grouped tautomeric molecules are visible
to the user to make further decisions.
There is no user account management or data security implemented in Sherlock’s
backend so far. Stored results are accessible without any login requirements. Hence, privacy
in terms of data does not exist yet. When planning to run Sherlock on a server or as a local
instance with multiple users, this must be considered. User-assigned data storage and access
via login methods are planned for future versions of Sherlock. [1]
Multiple ideas for potential future extensions have been described above. Due to the
microservice architecture of Sherlock’s backend, single services can be added to the network
if desired. This is a big advantage of a microservice architecture, next to the possibility of
integrating scalability tools if more computational power and instances of single nodes are
desired.
